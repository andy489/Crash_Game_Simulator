    Volatility Index — Explained

    The Volatility Index is the ratio VI = P90 / P50 of the crash-point distribution.

    P50 (median) — the crash point that exactly half of all rounds fall below.
    P90 — the crash point that 90% of rounds fall below; only the top 10% reach it or higher.

    The ratio tells you how many times larger the rare big crashes are compared to the typical one. For pure Pareto, P50 ≈ 2× and P90 ≈ 10×, giving VI ≈ 5.0 — always Medium.

    BandVI RangeWhat it means
    Low < 3.0 Tail barely wider than the median — P90 is less than 3× P50. Crash points cluster tightly; session results are predictable with modest swings.
    Medium 3.0 – 6.0 Moderate tail spread — P90 is 3–6× P50. Pure Pareto always lands here (VI ≈ 5.0): P50 ≈ 2×, P90 ≈ 10×. The expected outcome band for this simulator.
    High 6.0 – 12.0 Wide tail spread — P90 is 6–12× P50. Long losing streaks are common, punctuated by infrequent large wins.
    Extreme > 12.0 Very wide tail — P90 is more than 12× P50. Outcomes are dominated by rare jackpot-scale crashes. Session RTP can deviate significantly from the theoretical value.
    Computation: Each worker thread runs Knuth reservoir sampling (100 k samples) to estimate P50 and P90. After merging N threads: VI = mean(P90 threads) / mean(P50 threads). The ratio is insensitive to extreme outliers and to the overall mean level — it directly compares tail to centre of the distribution.
    Distribution Formula

    Every crash point is sampled from a pure Pareto distribution with survival function S(x) = P(crash ≥ x) = 1/x. This is the unique distribution that guarantees exactly 100% theoretical RTP for every cash-out multiplier — because cashOut × S(cashOut) = cashOut × 1/cashOut = 1.

    How a crash point is sampled (per spin)
    1 Draw a uniform random number u ∈ [0, 1) from the thread-local RNG.
    2 Apply the inverse-CDF: x = 1 / (1 − u).
    3 Cap the result at Max Multiplier. Return the crash point.
    Cost per spin: O(1) — one division + one min. Median: converges to 2.0×. Mean: converges to ln(Max). The cap reduces the theoretical mean but does not change the RTP — the win probability for any target t is exactly 1/t, so E[payout] = t × 1/t = 1.0 per unit staked.
    Median Estimation — Knuth Reservoir Sampling

    Computing an exact median requires sorting all values, which would mean storing every spin result in memory. For 500 000 000 spins × 8 bytes = 4 GB per thread — completely impractical. Instead, each thread maintains a fixed-size reservoir of 100 000 randomly-chosen values and estimates the median from that sample. Error stays well under 0.1% regardless of input size.

    Algorithm (per spin)
    1 Increment count (total spins seen so far).
    2 If count ≤ 100 000 — fill the reservoir sequentially. Every early spin is guaranteed a slot.
    3 Otherwise — pick a random integer j in [0, count).
    If j < 100 000, replace reservoir[j] with the new value; otherwise discard it.
    4 At the end, sort the reservoir and return the middle element (or average of the two middle elements).
    Why it works: At any point after the reservoir is full, each of the count values seen so far has exactly a 100 000 / count probability of being in the reservoir — a uniform random sample with no bias toward early or late values. This property was proven by Donald Knuth in The Art of Computer Programming (Algorithm R, Vol. 2).

    Memory: O(k) — fixed at 800 KB per thread regardless of spin count.  |  Time per spin: O(1) amortized — one RNG call and one possible array write.
